What is being demo'd?
The AMI development workflow and the creation of “Immutable Servers".
Why is this topic relevant?
I'll be describing some of the methods employed when developing AMI's.

This methodology can be adhered to by teams who wish to produce machine images (AMI’s) for any application, using a consistent, standardised means.
Why Immutable you ask?
  • Version Controlled Changes
  • Consistency
  • Scalability
  • No surprises!
  • Snowflake servers are a thing of the past

Immutable Servers

“By frequently destroying and rebuilding servers from the base image, 100% of the server's elements are reset to a known state, without spending a ridiculous amount of time specifying and maintaining detailed configuration specifications.” - Martin Fowler - ThoughtWorks

Who is the target audience?

Anyone who wants to produce version controlled AMI’s!
E.g. DevOps/Developers/Architects/Platforms Teams etc.

How?"Live Demo!"

How? Creating an AMI.


Upon "Packer Build" success regardless of the workflow task in use, a new AMI/Docker Image is produced.
Once you are satisfied and commit your change, a CI pipeline typically triggers a "Packer AMI Build" task and a new AMI is created on AWS.

How? Using your new AMI.


An AMI can be used in many ways. e.g. Currently we are using Terraform to manage our AWS GoCD farms.
When building/recreating a farm, reference is made to the new AMI ID. This image is deployed as an "Immutable Server" within a given farm.

How? Your new AMI & SSH Access?


In the case of our "GoCD farms”, we provide users staging SSH access if they need to do some hands on experimentation with their AMI.
As we are employing the immutable pattern, SSH access is NOT provided in the case of production.
How? AMI Layering.
A newly created AMI can be used as the basis for building another AMI.
Pain Points
